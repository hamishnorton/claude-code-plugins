For NZ primary school resources (Years 1–8), what are the best fonts and font settings (size, line spacing, letter spacing, alignment) for each year level, progressing from beginning readers to independent readers?

## Gemini

Designing resources for New Zealand primary schools requires balancing typography with cognitive development, accessibility, and the local curriculum. The transition from Years 1 to 8 marks a shift from "learning to read" (decoding individual letters) to "reading to learn" (absorbing complex information). 

Here is the best-practice guide for fonts and typographic settings for New Zealand primary school resources, progressing from beginning to independent readers.

---

### Universal Rules for All NZ Year Levels

Before diving into specific year levels, these baseline rules apply to **all** NZ classroom resources, heavily guided by the Ministry of Education (MoE) and Inclusive Education guidelines:

* **Macron Support:** This is non-negotiable. Any font used in NZ must cleanly support Te Reo Māori macrons (ā, ē, ī, ō, ū). If a font defaults to a different typeface for macronized letters, do not use it.
* **Alignment:** **Always left-align text** (ragged right). Justified text creates uneven gaps ("rivers of white space") that are incredibly disruptive to visual tracking, especially for dyslexic learners.
* **Emphasis:** Use **bolding** to draw attention. Avoid *italics*, ALL CAPS, and underlining (except for hyperlinks), as these distort the visual shape of the words and slow down reading speed.
* **Contrast:** Ensure high contrast between text and background. For digital screens or worksheets intended for neurodivergent learners, dark grey or black text on a soft, pastel, or off-white background is often preferred over stark white.

---

### Years 1–2 (Approx. Ages 5–6): Beginning Readers

Early readers rely heavily on letter shape recognition. The font should perfectly mirror how they are taught to write in the classroom, meaning "single-story" versions of the letters **a** and **g** are essential.

* **Best Fonts:** * **Playwrite NZ Basic:** Commissioned directly by the MoE to match the NZ handwriting curriculum. (Highly recommended).
    * **URW Gothic / Lexend:** Excellent, clean, geometric sans-serifs with single-story letters.
    * **Comic Sans / Comic Neue:** Despite its reputation in design circles, Comic Sans is widely praised by MoE Inclusive Education for early literacy and dyslexia support due to its distinct, unambiguous letterforms. Comic Neue is a free, refined alternative.
* **Font Size:** 18 pt to 24 pt. 
* **Line Spacing (Leading):** 1.5 to 2.0 (Double spacing). Young eyes need clear vertical lanes to track back to the left margin without skipping lines.
* **Letter Spacing (Tracking):** Slightly expanded. Giving letters a tiny bit of breathing room helps children isolate and decode individual sounds (phonemes).

### Years 3–4 (Approx. Ages 7–8): Early Fluent Readers

Students at this stage are building reading stamina. You can introduce standard "double-story" letters (the standard **a** and **g**) as they transition to reading conventional printed books.

* **Best Fonts:** Clean, highly legible sans-serifs. 
    * **Arial, Tahoma, Verdana, Carlito.** (Verdana is particularly strong here due to its wide letter spacing and tall lowercase letters).
* **Font Size:** 14 pt to 16 pt.
* **Line Spacing:** 1.5.
* **Letter Spacing:** Standard to very slightly loose.

### Years 5–6 (Approx. Ages 9–10): Fluent Readers

These students are reading chapter books and processing more complex worksheet instructions. The goal is to reduce visual fatigue over longer reading periods.

* **Best Fonts:** You can continue with standard sans-serifs for worksheets and UI, but this is the ideal time to introduce clean **serif** fonts for longer reading passages, as serifs help guide the eye along the baseline of the text.
    * *Sans-serif:* **Arial, Verdana, Roboto.**
    * *Serif:* **Georgia, Lora.** (Georgia is an excellent digital-first serif that remains highly readable).
* **Font Size:** 12 pt to 14 pt. (14 pt is still the recommended minimum for accessible/dyslexia-friendly documents).
* **Line Spacing:** 1.2 to 1.5.
* **Letter Spacing:** Standard.

### Years 7–8 (Approx. Ages 11–12): Independent Readers

At the intermediate level, students are preparing for the formatting expectations of high school and NCEA. Resources should mimic standard academic and publishing layouts, though readability remains key.

* **Best Fonts:** Standard academic typefaces. 
    * **Arial, Carlito, Georgia, Times New Roman.** (Note: The MoE's own corporate style relies heavily on Montserrat, which is also a clean, geometric option if you have access to it).
* **Font Size:** 11 pt to 12 pt. (12 pt is the standard requirement for most NZ academic and high-school assignment writing).
* **Line Spacing:** 1.15 to 1.5. (1.5 spacing is standard for drafted assignments to allow for teacher annotations).
* **Letter Spacing:** Standard.

---

### A Quick Note on Dyslexia and Inclusive Education

If you are creating resources for a mixed-ability classroom, it is always safest to default to **accessibility standards**. The New Zealand guidelines for inclusive education recommend maintaining a minimum font size of **12 pt to 14 pt**, using plain sans-serif fonts (like Arial or Verdana), and sticking to **1.5 line spacing** regardless of the year level. What works best for a dyslexic student will invariably make reading easier for the rest of the class as well.
